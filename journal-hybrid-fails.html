Why every hybrid office eventually fails — and the four moves that fix it.

Three years into the great hybrid experiment, most Singapore offices look the same as they did in 2019 — just less full. Post‑occupancy data from twelve TDI hybrid fit‑outs, and why the firms that designed for ambiguity outperform the ones that designed for headcount.

By Lin Jiahao · Design Director, TDI Workspace
·
Data: Aiko Tanaka, Workplace Strategy

The first thing you notice walking through a hybrid office on a Wednesday morning isn't the empty desks. It's the desks that are almost empty. A row of twelve workstations with three people at them, spaced as far apart as politeness allows. Headphones on. Cameras off. The room is laid out for forty‑eight; eleven are working. The space is not failing dramatically. It's failing quietly — by being exactly what it always was, only emptier.

For the past eighteen months we've been measuring twelve hybrid offices we built between 2022 and 2024, all in central Singapore, all between 5,000 and 14,000 sqft. We were curious about a specific question: which design moves correlated with healthy daily occupancy, and which didn't. The answer, when we ran the numbers, was not what we expected.

The dimensions we measured

For each space, we logged daily badge‑in data, surveyed staff at the 30, 60, 90 and 365‑day marks, and quantified six design variables: desk‑to‑seat ratio, focus‑room density, food & beverage offer, daylight depth, acoustic isolation, and the "third‑space" share of the floor. Then we plotted each office against three outcome metrics: average daily occupancy, employee satisfaction (Net Promoter Score), and number of complaints filed in months 4–12.

The single strongest predictor of daily occupancy across our sample wasn't desk count, free food, dog policy, ergonomic chairs, or even commute — variables we had assumed would matter. It was a single metric: the share of floor area that wasn't a desk and wasn't a meeting room. The cafés, the soft seating, the library nooks, the "I'll just sit here for a minute" spots. We've started calling this the third‑space share, and the higher it is, the more often people choose to come in.

The single strongest predictor of occupancy wasn't desks. It was the share of the floor that wasn't desks.

Why the typical hybrid floor fails

Most hybrid floors are designed by subtracting desks from a pre‑pandemic plan. A 9,000 sqft, 100‑desk floor gets cut to 60 desks for 100 hybrid staff. The remaining 40 desks become "additional" meeting rooms. Almost no third space. Almost no reason to come in, unless you'd been told to. The floor is technically smaller, but it feels emptier than it did when it had 100 people in it — because it's now optimised for nothing in particular.

The offices in our sample that scored highest on daily occupancy did the opposite. They cut not just desks but entire zones of desks, then put something else in their place. A long worktable that doubles as a meeting room. A library wall. A coffee bar facing the daylight. The desk count fell but the variety of places to sit went up. People came in because there were more reasons to come in, not because anyone had told them to.

The four moves that work

1. Cut desks by 35–45%, not 25%. The middle ground — taking out a quarter of the desks — is the worst place to land. You've lost density without gaining anything. The math only starts working when you free up enough floor to put something else there.

2. Aim for a third‑space share of 30%+. Below this number, our data shows daily occupancy struggles to break 50%. Above it, the curve climbs sharply. Soft furniture is cheaper per sqm than desking, so this is usually a budget‑positive change.

3. Put the daylight on the third space, not the workstations. The most universally‑loved spots in our sample were the soft seats nearest the windows. Workstations don't pull people in. Sunlight, coffee, and a soft chair do. Plan from the window inwards.

4. Design for ambiguity, not headcount. The single biggest predictor of failure in months 4–12 is over‑specification. A "marketing zone" for 12 marketers when there are only 8 marketers on a Tuesday is wasted real estate. Plans that work give people fewer named places and more reusable places.

Coda

None of this is to say desks don't matter. Heads‑down focus work still requires a desk, a screen, and a chair. But the question modern offices have to answer is no longer "where do I sit to work?" — it's "why am I here today?" If the building can't answer that for the average employee on the average Tuesday, the building loses to the kitchen table. The buildings that win are the ones that have, quite literally, designed in a reason to show up.

— Lin Jiahao & Aiko Tanaka, May 2026. Singapore.
